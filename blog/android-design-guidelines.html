Android Design Guidelines

The ultimate guide for designing modern mobile applications running Android.

Tweet

Android is one of the most popular platforms for designing and building applications. Tablets, phones, glasses and many other devices run Android OS. With such a great variety of screen sizes, resources and style guides designing for Android can be intimidating for a designer and a lot of resources are outdated.

Material Design brings consistency to Android design language. Below guideliens are meant to help a mobile designer to get familiar with common concepts and design patterns for Android.

The guide isn't meant to replace the official documentation by Google but rather help understand basic principles of designing for Android platform. Published: Nov 8, 2015.

Why is this important?

By following established patterns of the Android platform you can make the user feel comfortable using your app and the experience feel intuitive. It's User Experience Design, in a nutshell.

Units and measurements

Android runs on variety of devices with different resolutions. Because of many different screen sizes and resolutions, Android based operation system support a notion of Density-independent pixel (dp). Although the concept of density independent pixel has been around for a while there's still a lot of confusion around the difference between pixels, points and densities. Before we move forward let's make sure we have the perfect understanding of these terms.

Resolution, screen size and DPI explained

Resolution is the total number of physical pixels on a screen. Measured in pixels.

Screen size is physical size of the screen. Measured in inches. Grouped into 4 categories: small, normal, large, and extra-large.

Screen density is the amount of pixels on the screen within one inch. Measured in dpi (dots per inch) - number of dots (units) per screen inch. Grouped into six generalized densities: low, medium, high, extra-high, extra-extra-high, and extra-extra-extra-high.

DPI is an equivalent of one physical pixel on a 160 dpi screen. The conversion of dp units to screen pixels is simple: px = dp * (dpi / 160).

The generalized sizes and densities are arranged around a baseline configuration that is a normal size and mdpi (medium) density.

What does it mean for my design?

As you can see it's a good practice to start designing in @1x (scale factor 1) as a base line. Having more real estate on the screen can give application certain benefits but we'll talk about this in details below.

Adaptive design

It might look complicated at first but all you really need to do is to understand the basic concepts of adaptive design for mobile devices. The concept is similar to responsive design for web - we don't design for specific screen size - rather we choose a starting layout size and define breakpoints and how our design respond to hitting those breakpoints.

The basic design process can be outlined in following steps:

1. Choose a starting point. It's a good practice to research the market and understand what devices your product is used on. For example, if your primary market drivers in NYC - chances are they use the mobile phones vs. tablets which means you should be focused on mdpi-xhdpi screens first.

2. Design the UX for your main chosen point - screen density, orientation and device type. Make sure the UI is clear and intuitive but it also start thinking about how it's also scalable.

3. Define breakpoints and layout behavior. Based on a change in a real estate you can adapt your layout and either add more content or break it down into few screens where it makes sense.

With such a wide variety of screen sizes and densities a good rule of thumb is to design responsively starting at most commonly used device orientation and at normal density and scale up/down appropriately.

When designing for landscape orientation you might want to get advantage of extra screen width that you have.

  • Use dp when defining your application UI (instead of pixels). Make sure it scales properly on different screen sizes.

  • Provide alternative bitmap resources for different densities. For example, you should provide higher-resolution bitmaps for high-density screens and the system will use those instead of resizing the bitmap designed for medium-density screens.

Layout Components

Google came up with pretty specific rules on how designer should be treating the layout space. There are a few main points you should take into consideration when designing for Android.

Status bar

Purpose: Used for displaying system notifications, time and battery info.

On Android devices user has access to always visible status bar on top of the screen.

Visually the status bar may or may not be the same color as App bar depending on design considerations. I also find it's a good practice to make it black in certain cases to blend it with the device chrome.

The status bar can also be translucent.

App bar

Purpose: Special kind of toolbar. Used for displaying screen title/filters, navigation and available actions.

App bar is used to be called Action bar in the previous versions of Android.

App bar usually contains navigation elements, titles and actions. All of these are optional and depend on particular screen purpose and content.

The default height of App bar is 56dp in portrait and 48dp in landscape.

The following icons are commonly used in the App bar:

Menu opens up a side menu.

Back arrow takes user to the previous screen/acton or cancels the action being performed (exits search functionality).

Search launches search functionality.

Overflow menu opens up overflow menu sheet.

Submit as title suggests it submits the action - sends the message, proceeds to the next step in the flow, etc.

Refresh updates the current content on the screen with the latest data being displayed.

Heart bookmarks the current content item - photo, song, url, etc.

Trash bin performs the delete action.

... and many others.

When more than 3 action items are needed to be available in the app bar - they are placed in Overflow menu (see below).

  • App bar contains the most important actions in the current content that should be immediately available to the user. It's often most basic actions like - menu, navigation and actions important in the current context (send message).

  • Make sure to not clutter the App bar. 3-4 icons is more than enough, the rest can be placed in the Overflow menu.

In the App bar all icons and titles should be the same color. If you're using colorful background - usually white color is used for the icons. Make sure you maintain a good contrast between icons/titles and a background.

App bar can also be extended when you need to provide more space for branding or header image make the App bar more prominent. In this case the App bar height is multiplied by standard height (usually 4 times). Example:

Example could be the photo folder screen which contains photos grouped by specific criteria (destination).

Overflow menu

Purpose: Used to navigate to additional screens or access secondary actions. Usually hides under three dots icon on the top right.

Search

Purpose: Specific action that triggers search functionality within current context.

There are two search patterns supported by Android UI: persistent and expandable.

Persistent search is used when search functionality is the main focus of the app. Example: Yelp; user has to search for places (main action) to see places, read reviews, make reservations (following actions).

Expandable search is used when search isn't the main focus of the app but a nice-to-have functionality that allows users to search for content.

The magnifying glass icon on the right title is used to implement expandable search pattern. The title transforms into search field blinking cursor and back arrow to exit search. You can optionally include "x" icon to quickly clear the input field.

Tabs

Purpose: Serves for quick navigation and/or as a quick filter for displayed content. Tabs can also be used to change the way content is displayed (table vs list).

Tabs represent a clear navigation and provide context for a destination. Besides, the screens available from tabs are only one tap away vs. side nav that we will go over below. It's important to think through clear navigation patterns when designing your application.

Tabs are used as secondary level navigation as well when you're using side nav as primary navigation (see below).

Unlike iOS, on Android Tabs are always located on the top of the screen, often attached to the App bar. There are multiple reasons for it, one of them being Navigaion bar located on the bottom - so that two navigation elements do not conflict.

Tabs can also serve as quick selection filter on the current screen when user needs to switch between similar choices to see different results in the content area.

Tabs can optionally contain icons.

Side nav

Purpose: As title suggests used mainly for navigation the app. Can also contain info about user identity and help section.

Side nav (also known as Navigation Drawer) is a panel that is available by clicking on "hamburger" icon and slides from the left to reveal app navigation.

Side nav can contain sections for logical distintion between sections within your application.

  • Side nav is sophisticated way of presenting navigation. However do not overdo it. If you only have a few items to put in the navigation - more persistent pattern might be better. Remember - you job is to minimize the user efforts and eliminate amount of clicks if possible.

Bottom bar

Purpose: Specific type of toolbar that can be utilized for contextual actions, forward actions (Next), etc. Optional.

Bottom bar used to be extremely popular in Android design. Here is an example of how Jeremy Sallee, visual designer at Telly, utilized bottom bar for primary actions.

Another great use of bottom bar is editing menu.

However the bottom bar should be used carefully since its location is very close the navigation bar (see below). Consider user's reaction on accidentally navigation back simply because the actions located to close to Navigation bar.

Navigation bar

Purpose: A persistent pattern for Android devices regardless of brand. It contains Back, Overview and Recent actions.

You should account for this element in your app design.

Navigation bar can also be translusent or transparent:

The most important takeaway from having the persistent navigation bar is that you should avoid including bottom tab bars as navigation pattern in your application layout. Instead, it is sugested to use top bar or left menu as a way of navigation the app.

Content area

Purpose: Displaying the contextual data as intended by your application.

The content area is everything below the App bar and above the bottom bar. The content layout can be in a form of cards, timelines, lists, forms and many others. We'll go over different types of content areas in more details in the following posts.

Colors

Material Design guidelines from Google suggest using rich and bold colors when designing for Android. The usage of color should be determined by the branding guidelines and overall color atmoshere of the product you're designing for.

Color palette

You can refer to Google color palette when choosing colors for your application UI. Google suggests chosing 3 hues from primary palette as main colors and one from secondary palette (starting with A) as an accent color.

You can download official Google Material Design color swatches here.

Do I have to follow Material design color scheme?

It depends on the project. If you have an establish branding guidelines and color scheme to start off - you should utilize them in the application UI for creating visual consistency with other applications or products under the same brand.

However if you're starting from scratch having creative freedom - go ahead and create the color scheme using Material colors. They were carefully selected and tested by Google Design team. Using Material design suggested patterns will show that you as respect platform agnostic solutions and strive to minimize the learning curve for your users.

In any case - whether you follow establish color palletes or not - it is a good rule of thumb to check the color pairing suggestions.

Color guide

I always include color palette as a part of project style guide with reference for RBG and HEX colors as well as usage guidelines.

Typography

Roboto

Standard font used on Android devices is Roboto. You can download it here and use in your application design, it's an open type designed for high density screens.

Finally, typography uses a slightly finer baseline grid of 4dp, so line heights should be 20dp, 24dp, 32dp, 36dp, etc.

Roboto Mono

Noto

Noto has been the standard typeface on Android for all languages that are not covered by Roboto.

Custom fonts

You can include custom fonts into your Android application design to match the branding guidelines. The usage of custom font has to be justified by the need of the app and proven to be the better way of solving design problems that can't be solved with standard fonts offered by Android platform.

  • Use Roboto for Latin, Greek, and Cyrillic scripts. And Noto for all other languages.

  • Don't use too many text styles and sizes in your app design. It creates inconsistency and confuses the user.

Iconography

The sharp and recognizable icons are essential part of application UI. Make sure you icons communicating clear intention and get out of the way. It's a good idea to use Android system icons for common actions.

To make sure you accomodate for different screen sizes and resolutions, follow 2:3:4:6:8 scaling ratio when exporting your icon assets. For low-density screens Android will scale down the assets from HDPI size which is 1/2 of HDPI.

Launcher

It's the icon on the Home screen, tapping it will launch your application. It has to be sharp and have the level of details enough to recognize the application.

  • Start designing your icon from mdpi size to make sure it works for smaller screens. Then scale it up to accomodate hdpi, xhdpi, xxhdpi, xxxhdpi.

  • When designing icons use simple lines and recognizable pattertns. See if your icons are recognizable without titles or any other text next to it.
